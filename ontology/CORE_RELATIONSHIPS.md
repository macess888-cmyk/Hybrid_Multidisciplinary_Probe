# Core Relationships

## Purpose

This ontology defines the minimum relationships used to connect observations, claims, evidence, authority, execution, outcomes, and correction across the Hybrid Multidisciplinary Probe.

It does not declare a universal theory.

It provides a stable vocabulary for multidisciplinary inspection.

## Relationship Definitions

### Reality → Observation

Reality exists independently of the observer.

Observation is a bounded encounter with some aspect of reality.

Observation does not guarantee completeness or correctness.

### Observation → Record

A record preserves an observation in a transmissible form.

The record may lose context, timing, fidelity, or provenance.

### Record → Evidence

A record becomes evidence only relative to a defined question, claim, and review boundary.

Record existence does not establish evidentiary sufficiency.

### Evidence → Claim

Evidence may support, narrow, contradict, or leave a claim unresolved.

Claims do not inherit certainty merely because evidence exists.

### Claim → Verification

Verification tests whether a claim holds under declared conditions.

Verification does not establish universal truth beyond those conditions.

### Verification → Assessment

Assessment interprets verified and unresolved evidence for a defined purpose.

Assessment must preserve limitations and unknowns.

### Assessment → Admissibility

Admissibility determines whether the available evidence is sufficient for a proposed transition to proceed within a defined boundary.

Admissibility is not truth.

### Admissibility → Authority

An admissible proposal still requires valid authority before it may bind consequence.

Evidence sufficiency does not create authority.

### Authority → Binding

Binding attaches a valid authority and decision to a specific object, action, condition, time, and scope.

Authority without binding remains abstract.

### Binding → Execution

Execution performs the committed action.

A binding decision does not prove that execution occurred as approved.

### Execution → Outcome

Outcome records what followed execution.

Execution completion does not establish outcome success.

### Outcome → Revalidation

Revalidation tests whether assumptions, conditions, authority, and evidence remain current after execution or environmental change.

Previous validity does not carry forward automatically.

### Revalidation → Recovery

Recovery determines whether a system may safely resume, continue, or re-enter operation after interruption, failure, challenge, or changed conditions.

Alert withdrawal is not recovery.

### Recovery → Remedy

Remedy addresses identified consequences without rewriting the history under which they occurred.

Remedy is a new authority-bearing action.

### Remedy → Learning

Learning preserves validated observations, failures, corrections, and outcomes for future use.

Learning does not silently convert local findings into universal rules.

## Non-Equivalences

Reality != observation

Observation != record

Record != evidence

Evidence != truth

Evidence != authority

Claim != proof

Verification != universal validity

Assessment != permission

Admissibility != authority

Authority != binding

Binding != execution

Execution != intended execution

Execution != successful outcome

Outcome != recovery

Recovery != remedy

Remedy != historical revision

Consensus != convergence

Repetition != independent confirmation

Signed != true

Recorded != reality-correspondent

---

## Layer-Preservation Invariant

Each layer must preserve the limits of the layer before it.

A later layer may add authority, interpretation, or consequence.

It may not silently manufacture certainty, validity, or provenance that the previous layer did not establish.

---

## Unknown-State Rule

Where evidence is insufficient:

UNKNOWN → HOLD

HOLD preserves the question without promoting or rejecting the claim.
